A community sauna on the wild edge. Three names, three worlds: fire, sea and warmth.

Wood-fired heat on a January harbourside, a cold plunge in the Atlantic and Gaelic at the centre of it, not as decoration. Below are three complete directions. Each stands on its own, with its own logo, palette, type, voice and a working sample. Tagh aon. Choose one and I'll build the full system around it.

CONCEPT 01
Teine
/ˈtʃɛn.ə/ · "CHEH-nyuh"
Scottish Gaelic for fire
Rationale Teine is the hearth direction, all iron and ember. The mark is a single wind-leaned flame cut with the notch of a Cuillin peak, so heat and landscape read as one shape. A near-black peat ground lets the ember do all the talking and the raw grotesque wordmark feels worked, like something hammered rather than styled. It belongs on a slate sign, lit from below.

D · Voice
An ancient heat, kept for everyone.
Cùm an teine beò. "Keep the fire alive." An idiom for keeping the community and its traditions going.
The voice is plain and warm, like a harbourmaster who's glad you came. Sentences stay short. Nobody says "wellness". Heat and cold get equal billing.

CONCEPT 02
Cuan
/ˈkuən/ · "KOO-an"
Scottish Gaelic for ocean / sea
Rationale Cuan is the cold-water direction, with the plunge given top billing. The mark is a porthole holding two swells, the moment before you go under. A deep Atlantic ground and a high-contrast Caslon display make it the most editorial and maritime of the three, closer to a shipping line or a sea-swimming club than a sauna. One warm buoy-rust accent is the only heat in the frame, and it does a lot of work.

Into the blue
Body · IBM Plex Sans
Two minutes in the sea, ten in the heat and again. The water off Skye rarely climbs past nine degrees, which is rather the point.
High-contrast Caslon gives an editorial, slightly nautical authority, the feel of old chart engraving. IBM Plex Sans is the modern, technical counterweight for tide tables and times. Both free on Google Fonts.
D · Voice
Come for the cold. Stay for the heat.
Thig don chuan. "Come to the sea." An open invitation rather than a command.
The voice is spare and a little salty, measured and weatherwise and quietly proud of the cold. Numbers and degrees are welcome. It stays calm and never reaches for hype.

CONCEPT 03
Blàths
/blaːs/ · "blaas"
Scottish Gaelic for warmth / blossom
Rationale Blàths is the human, hospitable direction, warmth as a feeling rather than a temperature. The mark is a sprig of bog cotton (canach an t-slèibh), the white tuft that blossoms across Highland moors in summer. It is soft, local and a world away from spa lotus motifs. The delicate high-contrast Instrument Serif gives it an unhurried, almost handwritten editorial grace, warmed by amber and dusty heather. It feels like being welcomed in from the weather.

Instrument Serif is delicate and high-contrast, editorial without being stiff and almost spoken aloud. Work Sans grounds it with friendly, even body text. Both free on Google Fonts.
D · Voice
Warmth, the way the island gives it.
Blàths à fuachd. "Warmth out of cold." The heat the plunge gives back.
The voice is gentle and generous and speaks as "we". "Come in", "sit with us", "there's tea after". Hospitable without being soft. It welcomes the weather rather than hiding from it.
